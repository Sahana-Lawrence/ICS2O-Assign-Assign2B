  1. After her graduaton in 1945, Jean Bartik was hired to compute ballistic firing tables for the U.S. Army.
  2. When the University of Pennsylvania completed a novel electronic device to compute firing tables automatically, she was chosen, along with five other women, to work on the new machine called ENIAC.
  3. The team, in which Bartik was included, taught themselves ENIAC's operation and became its, and the world's, first programmers.
  4. In 1947, Bartik became a part of a group that converted ENIAC into a stored-program computer. This was a major milestone that improved ENIAC's efficiency and usefulness.
  5. Bartik also made contributions to the early BINAC and UNIVAC 1 computers.
  6. Jean Bartik became a strong advocate for increased participation by women in science and technology.

1931-1943

Grace started teaching Mathematics to students at Vassar after receiving her master's degree. She continued to teach while obtaining her doctorate and for years to come, eventually getting promoted to Associate Professor in 1941 at the age of 35.

1943

Grace was granted a leave from Vassar in order to join the United States Navy Reserve WAVES(Women Accepted for Volunteer Emergency Service) program during World War II.

1944

Grace was asigned to work on the Bureau of Ships Computation Project at Harvard. There she worked under Howard Aiken programming the Mark 1 computer. They used it to solve problems for the war effort, including ones for the Manhattan Project.

1945

Problems with the Mark I led to the discovery that a moth was disrupting the machine. Grace logged, "First actual case of bug being found". This encouraged the use of the term computer bug.

1949

After the war Grace began working for Eckert–Mauchly Computer Corporation. She was a senior member on the team that developed UNIVAC computers. UNIVAC became the most widely used and well known computers of the 1950s.

1952

While working for Eckert–Mauchly Grace invented and built the first operational compiler for a computer.

1959

Hopper assisted in the development of the COBOL(COmmon Oriented Business Language) programming language.

1967-1986

Grace joined the Navy and with the exception of a few short retirements remained an active duty officer into her late 70s. During this time she helped write validation software for COBOL and worked to develop standards for software and computing systems.

1991

Having already achieved the rank of Commandore(later named Rear Admiral) while in the Navy, Grace received the National Medal of Technology in recognition of her many contributions to the computing field.

Jean Bartik played an important role in the history of computer science, and she has earned many awards in her lifetime such as, 'Inductee, Women in Technology International Hall of Fame (1997)', 'Fellow, Computer History Museum (2008)', 'IEEE Computer Pioneer Award, IEEE Computer Society (2008)', and Korenman Award from the Multinational Center for Development of Women in Technology (2009)'.
