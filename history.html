History

Jean Jennings Bartik, born Betty Jean Jennings in Gentry County, Missouri, in 1924, was the sixth of seven children. Her Father, William Smith Jennings was from Alanthus Grove, where he was a schoolteacher, as well as a farmer. Her mother, Lula May Spainhower, was also from Alanthus. Bartik had three older brothers, William, Robert, and Raymond; two older sisters, Emma and Lulu, and one younger sister, Mable. In her childhood, Bartik rode on horseback to visit her grandmother, who bought her a newspaper to read every day and became a role model for Bartik for the rest of her life. Jean Bartik began her education in a one-room school, and she gained local attention for her skill in softball. In order to attend high school in the neighbouring town, she lived with her older sister, and she began to drive every day despite being only 14 years old. She graduated from Stanberry High School in 1941 at the age of 16. After high school, Bartik attended Northwest Missouri State Teachers College, now known as Northwest Missouri State University, and she majored in mathematics with a minor in English, and she graduated in 1945. Bartik was awarded the only mathematics degree in her class.She decided to study mathematics instead of journalism as she'd originally intended to do, because she had a bad relationship with her advisor. Later in her life, Bartik earned a master's degree in English at the University of Pennsylvania in 1967 and she was awarded an honorary doctorate degree from Northwest Missouri State University in 2002.

1906

Grace Hopper was born December 6, 1906 in New York City. She was the oldest of three children. As a child she was known for her natural curiosity and for what she would later call her love of gadgets.

1928

Grace recieved her Bachelors degree in Mathematics and Physics from Vasser in 1928. She then went on to study at Yale where she graduated with her Masters degree in Mathematics in 1930 and her Ph.D. in 1934.

1931-1943

Grace started teaching Mathematics to students at Vassar after receiving her master's degree. She continued to teach while obtaining her doctorate and for years to come, eventually getting promoted to Associate Professor in 1941 at the age of 35.

1943

Grace was granted a leave from Vassar in order to join the United States Navy Reserve WAVES(Women Accepted for Volunteer Emergency Service) program during World War II.

1944

Grace was asigned to work on the Bureau of Ships Computation Project at Harvard. There she worked under Howard Aiken programming the Mark 1 computer. They used it to solve problems for the war effort, including ones for the Manhattan Project.

1945

Problems with the Mark I led to the discovery that a moth was disrupting the machine. Grace logged, "First actual case of bug being found". This encouraged the use of the term computer bug.

1949

After the war Grace began working for Eckert–Mauchly Computer Corporation. She was a senior member on the team that developed UNIVAC computers. UNIVAC became the most widely used and well known computers of the 1950s.

1952

While working for Eckert–Mauchly Grace invented and built the first operational compiler for a computer.

1959

Hopper assisted in the development of the COBOL(COmmon Oriented Business Language) programming language.

1967-1986

Grace joined the Navy and with the exception of a few short retirements remained an active duty officer into her late 70s. During this time she helped write validation software for COBOL and worked to develop standards for software and computing systems.

1991

Having already achieved the rank of Commandore(later named Rear Admiral) while in the Navy, Grace received the National Medal of Technology in recognition of her many contributions to the computing field.
